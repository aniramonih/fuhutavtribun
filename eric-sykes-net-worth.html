Eric Sykes Overview and Wiki

Eric Sykes, born on May 4, 1923, in Oldham, Lancashire, began his entertainment career during World War II while serving in the Royal Air Force. His early work included collaborating with fellow servicemen on troop entertainment shows.

After the war, he moved to London, where he quickly established himself as a comedy writer, working with Bill Fraser and Frankie Howerd.

Transition to Television and Radio

His transition from radio to television began in the early 1950s. He wrote for various BBC series, including The Howerd Crowd and Frankie Howerd’s Korean Party. His first screen appearance was in the army film comedy Orders Are Orders (1954).

Around 1953, he shared an office with Spike Milligan, and they later co-founded Associated London Scripts, a writers’ agency.

Collaboration with Spike Milligan

He collaborated with Spike Milligan on scripts for The Goon Show, easing Milligan’s workload. Their partnership included writing for the special Archie in Goonland and several episodes of The Goon Show itself.

Success with ‘Sykes and a…’

In 1960, He co-starred with Hattie Jacques in the BBC TV sitcom Sykes and a…, which he created in collaboration with writer Johnny Speight. The show was a hit, establishing ‘Eric and Hat’ as a popular comedy duo. Sykes wrote most of the episodes, introducing his famous slapstick routine The Plank.

Film Career

Sykes’ film career included roles in Village of Daughters (1962) and Kill or Cure (1962).

He also appeared in Those Magnificent Men in Their Flying Machines (1965) and The Liquidator (1965). In 1967, he expanded his routine into the short film The Plank, which featured several comedy stars.

Later Career and Television Specials

Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Sykes continued to work in television and film. He appeared in Theatre of Blood (1973) and wrote and starred in various TV specials, including Eric Sykes Shows a Few of Our Favourite Things (1977) and Rhubarb Rhubarb (1980).

He also played roles in The Boys in Blue (1982) and If You Go Down in the Woods Today (1981).

Final Years and Legacy

In his later years, Sykes appeared in Gormenghast (2000), The Others (2001), and Harry Potter and The Goblet of Fire (2005). He also had roles in Last of the Summer Wine and New Tricks in 2007. His autobiography, If I Don’t Write It, Nobody Else Will, was published in 2005.

He passed away on July 4, 2012, leaving behind a legacy as a beloved figure in British comedy.

Personal life
