Tragic Death of Jonathan Cross, American Airlines Employee, In Plane Engine Accident

The aviation industry and the general public have been deeply shocked by the rare and horrific accident that claimed the life of Jonathan Cross, an American Airlines employee who tragically lost his life after being ingested into a plane engine.

According to search results, Jonathan Cross, a 28-year-old worker hailing from the Bronx, New York, was engaged in routine maintenance on an Embraer E175 aircraft’s engine at John F. Kennedy International Airport on October 21, 2023. In a tragic turn of events, the engine unexpectedly powered on, resulting in a fatal accident that led to his untimely demise. Tragically, he was pronounced dead at the scene.

The incident is currently under investigation by the National Transportation Safety Board (NTSB), the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA), and American Airlines. The NTSB has indicated that the aircraft was parked at the gate with the parking brake engaged when the accident transpired. Concurrently, the FAA has expressed its intent to review the airline’s procedures and training protocols for ground personnel.

This unfortunate event is not the first of its kind, as similar accidents have occurred involving ground workers. In January 2023, a Piedmont Airlines worker, a regional subsidiary of American Airlines, lost their life in a comparable accident at Montgomery Regional Airport in Alabama. Additionally, in June 2023, a Delta Air Lines employee also tragically passed away in an accident at San Antonio International Airport in Texas, which was subsequently determined to be a suicide by the medical examiner.

These incidents have raised significant concerns regarding the safety of ground workers and the imperative need to prevent such accidents. Some experts have recommended the implementation of additional safety measures and warnings to deter personnel from approaching active engines. This may include the utilization of sensors, alarms, or physical barriers. Others advocate for enhanced communication and coordination between pilots, ground crews, and air traffic controllers to minimize the risk of human errors and miscommunications.
